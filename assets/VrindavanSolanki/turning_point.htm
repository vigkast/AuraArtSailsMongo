The Turning Point

From carefree meanderings in the Gir forest, from the dusty pagdandis of Junagadh, a lad influenced by poetry tore himself away from the goatherds and banjarans, and made his way to Bombay, seeking there his gurukul of art. The city was a cocoon, nurturing his chrysalis of talent. A talent for line and form that was recognized at first flight. What won the young artist recognition and awards, and eventually a place among the stalwarts, was his ability to capture a luminescence out of the deftly spun hatchwork, glowing through his crisscross of black lines on canvas, that reflected the spiritual purity of his folk subjects.

But Mumbai’s influence on Vrindavan, though subtle, went deep. Preconditioned by a rural sensibility that was already shaping the future forms in his work, he was unaware that there was an urban strand to the double helix that was responding to this external environment. His perception had elided off the cityscape and asphalt jungle, but paused to marvel at the felt ambience of this sprinkling of magnificent edifices nestling within Mumbai’s central core. The young Solanki, trying to root himself in an alien environment, formed a lasting attachment to these gothic structures, seeing in them an art-reality. The buildings were objective, solid, seemingly eternal; in a city of flux, they promised security and continuity to this beginner.

Mumbai’s formative influence on Solanki was not to be acknowledged for years – till its ultimate surfacing in this show. The paintings here are not a passing nod, nor a dutiful salute, and not a prayerful obeisance – what one sees in them is a complete out pouring of affectionate respect. And gratifyingly, one sees the willingness of a mature artist to dare to break new ground, to re-invent himself. This effort resounds with the joy of release that young Vrindavan must have felt when effecting his first transition decades ago.

This turning point is marked in time and place - with the start of a new millennium that forces one to turn one’s back on one’s past work to face the challenge of the future. And the place itself, transformed in name from Bombay to Mumbai, re-invigorated by fresh winds of global change, re-defining itself. Solanki too marks out totally uncharted co-ordinates in his artistic geography. And from this new standpoint, he takes sightings of the oft-visited old places and street faces, and plunges ahead into new territory. What we see are unexpected and bold strides in direction: tints and tones enriching what used to be pen and line, shadows in shapes, subtle hues and shades, unconventional techniques applied to conventional facades.

Solanki has arrived at a decisive new platform – in figure, form and colour. As infrared to ultraviolet and colours in between combine into what the eye sees as light, Solanki’s oeuvre is not one that polarizes or refracts, but one that synthesizes the essence and enlightens, not in a coldly scientific mode, but in a warm humanism that shines through in every work.

Solanki’s artistic journey has always been, in a certain sense, a pilgrim’s progess. This turn in his artistic journey has fellow travelers that he acknowledges in a novel way. He mixes in the shades of dry pastels on much like the bhelwala concocts his recipe of colour. Each visually entrancing prospect of a Mumbaikar encountered – from dabbawala to patiwala – is set down in earthy tones, and together they present a microcosm of Mumbai. Solanki bridges his rural-urban divide with a simultaneous leap into hues and shades hitherto taboo. No longer are they a distraction in his homage to deified tradition, but a means to his joyous liberating participation in the reality of the urban spectrum.

These commoner’s lives are interlinked in a daisy-chain of interdependence, of Solanki's identifying them as the indefatigable bedrock spirit of Mumbai is evident in each portrayal. The acrylics, pastels and etchings taken together form a unified base, the edifices and people resonating off each other. Much as the stone structures impose on one’s consciousness, these faceless unidentified people are to Solanki, as always, the sap on which the city lives and has its being.

In the artist’s earlier works, his “faceless faces” were not a lack of recognition, but due to a sense of reverence, an unwillingness to limn the divine. But whether bangdis or odhni, every stroke of the overall imagery communicated a compelling description of his subject’s identify that no detail of nose or turn of ear could capture. Solanki’s canvases shared glimpses of his eternal people sealed in an age-old identity of subscribed tradition. Turning away his point of focus would have been traumatic if it were sudden. Bound as he was by the umbilical cord of joined identity, he would take a couple of years after the initial thought was broached, to acknowledge the undeniable influence of an art-nurturing city. There was much introspection before the rural strand in the helical turn admitted the existence within his artistic DNA of the urban genome. And yet as each gene relates to a feature or trait that manifests in maturity, so too in the fullness of time arrived these expressions. Now finally, Solanki lets us see both figures and features, perhaps just hinted at, but the very vagueness purposefully underlining the everyman nature of his subject.

Aficionados of Solanki’s work – and those who may have been so far outside this sizeable coterie – will need to re-assess the artist in this fresh revelation of his acknowledged talent. The stretched new canvas is a portent of an exciting phase – not face-less or hue-less any more. The genie, one might say, is out of the bottle. An earlier obsessive rural bias has been provided soft release. Given his undoubted skill, his eye for composition and form, the deftness of hand evident in each stroke, we can hope that this crossover will, going forward, build on the augmented creative strengths this show has uncovered.


- Eric & Chitralekha Martin
