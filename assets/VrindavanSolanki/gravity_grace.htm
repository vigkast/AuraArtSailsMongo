Gravity and Grace

A vista of rural men and women, faceless entities with innate expressions drawn and painted in black and white, adorning elaborately patterned costumes and intricately designed jewelry, busily engaged in their daily routine or simple social milieu: these have continued to be recurring themes in the vast repertoire of master artist Vrindavan Solanki. This together-with evocative drawings and paintings of the mountainous landscapes, suburban views of the Saurashtra region, and architectural landmarks of the city of Mumbai. Such have been the artist’s playing fields over the last four decades. They present an interesting collage of his artistic sojourns marking some amazing innate creations.

Re-tracing the Roots
The roots of Vrindavan Solanki’s artistic oeuvre, ethnic in its resonance, reach back to Junagadh in rural of Gujarat of pre-Independence India, where he was born and where he spent his childhood growing up in a middle-class joint family of coppersmiths. With meager resources and second youngest amongst six siblings, Solanki showed little interest in family matters or academic pursuits, preferring instead to spend his time idling around, going for solitary long walks in the forest amidst nature, reading and writing poetry, working in theatre, drawing and sketching, or helping his over-worked mother with household chores. Quiet and shy by nature, he preferred the silence of the woods or the solitude of his home, to playing games like the rest of lads in his neighbourhood.

With no artistic lineage to boast of, Solanki’s life and the track of his creative work have gone through many twists and turns. He had to struggle hard to fend for himself, throughout his student years and the early stages of his career. In his personal life, though, the “happy-go-lucky” fellow who believes in fate, has been fortunate enough to find his soul-mate and wife in Chitra. They left Junagadh over two decades ago in search of better opportunities and relocated themselves in the city of Ahmedabad. This has continued to be their base since then, and this is where they live with their son Bhishma, now a young man. This peaceful and contented personal graph gets mirrored in the artist’s creative outpouring that is bereft of any suggestions of struggle or violence or politics. Instead, it offers delight and solace through its earthy, simple appeal endowed with beauty and sensuality.

Following the advice of one of his teachers, Solanki first joined the J.J. School of Art in Mumbai. Though he did well in his exams he found the course too academic for his practical turn of mind, and the city of Mumbai too impersonal and expensive to live in. His lack of English and reserved nature made him feel a misfit in the metropolis. So he left for Baroda to pursue his studies at the M.S. University there. Given his talent, he was admitted to the painting course straightaway. But the success of his first show in Mumbai, while he was still in the last year of his diploma course in painting at Baroda, lured him away from his studies. He began to focus his energies on creating drawings and paintings that he sold to discerning collectors, returning to the college only ten years later, not only to complete his studies for a diploma in painting, but also to take up a post-diploma course in graphics.

Having tasted success at an early stage, when he sold his first two paintings for six hundred rupees on the opening day of his first-ever solo show at the Jehangir Art Gallery in Mumbai in 1969 to a discerning collector, Solanki has not had to look back since then. Successive exhibitions, commissions and a five-year stint of teaching art at the National Institute of Design in Ahmedabad gave him the much-needed financial security, besides enhancing his self – confidence. Socially committed, Solanki’s track record also includes several artistic initiatives and cultural festivals that he organized, such as an Artist’s Workshop focusing on the city’s heritage for the Ahmedabad Municipality, and a group exhibition with young artists for an Inside – Outside mega show. The accomplished artist’s work has featured in a text book, which is taught as a part of the 10th class curriculum for Gujarat state Education Board students.

Innate Expressions
Solanki’s imagery, serene and rustic on the one hand, reverberates with innate expressions on the other, evoking a feeling of eternity and timelessness. There are paintings featuring single figures, or couples or group of people. While their heads are often covered partially or fully with a pallu, chunri, chaddar or pagadhi, his figuration is characterized by omission of details on the face. Resorted to for dramatic effect, it communicates despite the absence of facial features. A closer, prolonged look reveals a myriad hues and patterns on the bedecked finery of men and women of the shepherd tribe engaged in chatting, or busy sharing their joys and sorrows, or recalling legends.

The imagery of village folk and scenes of his native Junagadh and surrounding towns seem to flow naturally from Solanki’s brush. Attired in block-printed sarees, and ghagras and amazingly crafted jewellery, the women appear graceful and sensuous, not erotic, and the men in their stately dhotis, and pagadhis, self-composed. A filial, benevolent atmosphere pervades works where mother and child make a appearance in delicate renderings. His nomadic characters appear aloof but self-assured, even as they struggle to eke out in living on the periphery of today’s urban sprawl.

There are other compositions where the figures, standing, sitting, or squatting, singly or huddled in groups, at times with their backs to the viewer, are seen gazing into the distance, adding a palpable dimension of suspense to his otherwise static, anonymous portraiture. The swirling ghaghras, hugging cholis, embroidered jackets, tie-and-die odhnis and voluptuous head-gear provide rich imagery, while their twinkling anklets, colourful bangles and tattooed arms tell their own stories.

His mother-and-child series are delicate portrayals of maternal love whether in repose, or engaged in household chores. Two large, striking oil paintings, in this genre, dominate the current show. Solanki’s creative versatility extends to evocative drawings of landscapes and cityscapes including landmark buildings and city scenes of Mumbai, Ahmedabad and Baroda etc with details of arches, balconies, carved canopies, the like of many of which have since been reduced to dust.

Method & Matrix
Solanki works in a wide range of media: oil and acrylic on canvas, drawings on paper, and graphics: etchings. Lithographs and screen prints. A conscientious and meticulous artist, he starts regularly at six in the morning and goes on until late in the evening each day of the week. Working as he does on more than one painting at any given time, his is slow process which takes him weeks to complete one small work. Solanki talks to his canvas as one would to a companion, even as he claims that he is not a very social or outgoing type. This however seems somewhat misplaced if one considers the number of workshops and interactive sessions he has organized for young artists and art students to make them aware of their environment and roots.

Preferring to work in series, his repository includes drawings and paintings of simple folk of the region as well and landscapes Saurashtra, the rocks and forest of Girnar, and Bombay cityscapes. “People fascinate me”, says the artist. On his obsession with drawing faces without features he explains, “People are two faced – inner and outer” and rather than show their one face and not the other he draws faceless people or featureless faces “I see no need of these on the face”. For him their body language says its all. Each painting captures a different persona encased within his/her frame of mind. There are images of Rabari women, milk maids, Girnar hills, surrounding forests and familiar cityscapes with their architectural features. ‘Romancing the line’ the title of another one of his earlier solo shows highlights his focus on line work in drawings as well as paintings.

Meticulous planning and hard work go into each work. The artist first makes a sketch; then visualizes it on a larger scale. As the white canvas stares at him, he wonders, “What should be around the figure? Should I create a black patch or leave it just like that, as a pure white halo?” Leaving some portions of the canvas blank in white “becomes an active part in my work as it allows the light to flow in”, and here the artist refers not just the day light "but also an inner spiritual light". His fine lines and realistic renderings add a magic, a sensuality and earthiness to the energy and appeal of his figuration.

Solanki’s people can be seen engaged in social inter-action, be they men, women or children. It is the delicacy and the linear quality of a woman’s figure that the artist finds irresistible, while for men it is their strength or physique that he likes to focus on. In portraying children he concentrates on their innocence. In all cases his human form is marked by the delicacy and dynamic movement in bold line work. Even when seated, his people, their pallus or dhotis flowing with the wind, seem animated as they engage in conversation. A serene mood is created through fine craftsmanship and it is this mood that pervades the featureless faces of people in his works. Solanki’s works are mostly black-and-white renderings, though sepia and brown are occasionally added for detailing in some of the works. It is the black rocks of the Saurashtra mountains and dark forest of the Girnar region that seem to creep into his landscape imagery. ”That contrast has lived in my mind and gets reflected in my figures and landscapes”. On his limited use of colour the artist says, “I never felt left out for wanting to add color………… all possible colours are absorbed in my shades of black and white”, though he might go in for colour one day if the need arises. The use of oil-bars that he has been experimenting with on recent years was triggered off by a visit to New York where he bought them. The first resulting work on canvas, which included browns and a limited use of pastel shades, was a gift to his wife.

For Solanki the practice of art is a spiritual experience, an act of devotion. In his view,”drawing is crucial, and I make variations in my practice, sometimes using a brush and at others charcoal”. Beginning with simple sketches and line drawings , etched gradually in deft strokes and measured smudging, his work graduates to larger images and complete expressions in figurative paintings and drawings. Revelling in his roots and working in hatched lines on canvas, a chiaroscuro effect is created to lend mood and character to the imagery, where not just the portrait, but also the fabric it adorns, holds it own in a balanced compositional grid. There is an omission of more decorative elements and incorporation of abstraction in his later and more matured work though the figure has continued to be both his main obsession and nemesis.

Though open to such innovation and change as his own work has undergone over the years, as his compositions in oval form, for Solanki art must belong to the soil. He does not believe in art that blindly imitates the west or Lacks roots. Apart from sketches, drawings and paintings of figures, landscapes and cityscapes, Solanki was commissioned to do the mural for the Air-India office in New York. His detailed drawings and paintings of Mumbai and Ahmedabad, landmarks of the 1980s and 90s, constitute important historical documentation apart from beautiful imagery. A similar series resulting from a study tour of Jamshedpur, at the invitation of the Tata Group, was featured in their annual calendar for the year 1991.

Line after line, layer over layer filling, overlapping and smudging, the artist has been able to create striking chiaroscuro effects with his play of light-and-shade in black and white. Noteworthy is the whiteness of moon-beams reflected through trees in one of his monochromatic works rotating around Girnar, and the changing seasons and moods they evoke. Diligently worked lines and hatching create a realistic and dreamy aura around the work. Solanki also uses rabbit-glue and gesso to prepare the surface of his canvas for added technical flexibility. With a mix of charcoal, pen-and ink, oil, acrylic and blow-torch technique on wood, Solanki’s featureless faces begin to assume a distinctive character. Bamboo sticks traditionally used by traders, and nibs that he got familiar with in America, are his favorite tools. Watered down acrylic is used to make its consistency more liquid.

The prolific artist, whose enormous repertoire has been exhibited in nearly two hundred shows throughout India and internationally, has several awards to his credit and is a collector’s delight. Though, like his contemporaries, the artist is happy for his work to be seen, admired and collected, when the time comes to part with it, he says, “On the last day of my show, as the works come down, I begin to feel an emptiness creeping inside me and my eyes turn moist”. At such moments, the sketch book which is Vrindavan’s constant companion brings him solace and recall. There are numerous drawings, large and small, made over the years that add to his repertory, encompassing people, landscapes, urban cityscapes, objects and events. Though so far the artist has kept his figurative works and landscapes apart in two distinct domains, it would be interesting to see in what stream his creativity flows if he were to extend his subject matter into a wider art-scape, or include people in his landscapes and some of the interiors or architectural features in his figuration.

To sum up: a distinctive feature of Vrindavan Solanki’s artistic philosophy is its rustic appeal. His images, in whatever genre, are bonded to his environment and emanate from his own experiences and shared interests with people he has lived his life with. There is grace and stateliness in his folk, be they Rabari men, women or children; or Kolis and other tribals, in the way they stand, sit, squat or walk, talk and conduct themselves. The beauty and grace of his women in their noble appearance, richly embroidered garments and their jholas offer a contrast to his men. A different sort of ambience is featured in his renderings of picturesque mountains, dark forests and quiet nature-scapes. There are intimate sketches of shepherds, their rural habitats, city-scapes, events and objects. With a turn of the head, or the positioning of their torsos, or grace in their walk, they exude a serenity encased in beauty and sensuality. The work in the exhibition includes oil and acrylic paintings, drawings and graphics of people and their meanderings in the comfort of their familiar surroundings. Their tight fitting cholis, flowing ghagras, patterned sarees, elegant veils or majestic turbans swirl, sway, conceal and reveal their mood and character to make the current show a suite of impressive innate expressions.

Sushma K Bahl MBE is an independent arts consultant, writer and curator of cultural projects based in Delhi. A Trustee and Advisory Panel Member of some select cultural and educational institutions in India and abroad, she headed the Arts and Culture Department of the British Council India until 2003. She was Guest Director for the XI Triennale-India 2005, and has curated several exhibitions on contemporary Indian art and authored/edited a selection of art books and catalogues.
